Context

What happens to health scores when metabolites are removed

This report measures the effect of removing metabolite markers from the panel, using the biomarker-to-process mappings behind the report. For every patient, we recomputed health scores twice, once with the full panel and once with metabolites removed, at three levels: the 12 diseases, the 7 body systems, and cancer risk.

Among patients who are already flagged as at-risk, health scores move enough to flip 27% of them into "looks optimal" on average across the 12 diseases, and up to 79% for the hardest-hit body system, with no change in their actual biology. Population-wide, scores barely move. See Diseases, Body systems, and Cancer risk.
Section 1

What's actually in the panel

140 distinct protein markers and 259 distinct metabolite markers are tested and reported. Only 81 of the 259 tested metabolites feed into one of the 37 processes the disease and system logic in this report is built on. The next three sections measure how much losing them moves health scores, at the disease, system, and cancer-risk level.

Section 2

Impact on the 12 diseases

Diseases are made up of a group of biomarkers: each of the 12 diseases maps to a set of processes, and each process maps to a set of markers. For every patient, we recomputed their health score for each disease using every mapped marker they have results for, then again with metabolites removed.

At-risk patients, defined here as the worst-scoring 25% of the cohort for that disease under the full panel (a health-score percentile, not a yellow or red flag), show a much larger effect than the population as a whole. Across the 12 diseases, this group's health score lift averages 3.1 times the population-wide lift, and for several diseases, at least 39% of at-risk patients see their health score cross into optimal territory (≥90) once metabolites are dropped.

Averaged across everyone, health scores move far less: 0.1 to 2.5 points depending on the disease, because metabolites flag out of range more often and more severely than proteins do, so dropping them removes some of the drag on the population-wide average.

Marker-count share doesn't fully predict the damage. Composition percentages are a reasonable first filter; they aren't a substitute for measuring the score effect directly.
Section 3

Impact on the 7 body systems

Each of the 7 body systems maps directly to a set of markers. We ran the same health score comparison at the system level: full panel vs. metabolites removed, population-wide and among the worst-scoring quarter of the cohort for each system.

Digestion & gut health (15% protein) and energy & metabolism (2% protein) are the most affected: 79% and 72% of their at-risk patients cross into "optimal" once metabolites are dropped. Immune system & inflammation and tissue repair & healing, both 100% protein, show no effect at all.

Section 4

Impact on cancer risk

Cancer risk uses its own biomarker map, organized into 8 cancer-relevant processes (angiogenesis, cell proliferation, immune system evasion, matrix remodelling, metabolic dysfunction, metastasis, oxidative stress, and thromboinflammation) rather than the 37-process framework used for diseases and systems. We ran the same comparison across all 90 mapped cancer-risk markers, and again within each of the 8 processes.

Cell proliferation (9% protein) and oxidative stress (17% protein) are the most affected: 88% and 84% of their at-risk patients cross into "optimal" once metabolites are dropped. Matrix remodelling, metastasis, and thromboinflammation, each at least 80% protein, show almost no effect.

Verdict

The effect concentrates hard in specific places

The effect concentrates hard in specific diseases, body systems, and cancer processes. In the hardest-hit group, up to 88% of at-risk patients would score as optimal instead of at-risk, with no change in their actual biology. Population-wide, health scores barely move across diseases, systems, and cancer risk alike, so the effect stays invisible in an average and only shows up once at-risk patients are isolated.

Alzheimer's disease, PCOS, type 2 diabetes, depression, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, energy & metabolism, digestion & gut health, hormone & brain function, cell proliferation, and oxidative stress all sit above 40% crossing. Rheumatoid arthritis, COPD, atherosclerosis, cardiovascular disease, immune system & inflammation, tissue repair & healing, matrix remodelling, metastasis, and thromboinflammation all sit under 10%.

Recommendations:

1. Energy & metabolism, digestion & gut health, and the five diseases and cancer processes above 40% crossing should be treated as high-risk for a protein-only version of the report. Exclude them from its claims, or supplement with a subset of the 179 unmapped metabolites, since a large share of already-flagged patients in these groups would otherwise read as optimal.

2. Composition percentage alone is a weak predictor of impact. PCOS and cell proliferation both show a bigger score effect than their protein share suggests. Any disease, system, or process under consideration for a protein-only version of the report should have its score effect measured directly, not estimated from marker counts.

3. The groups that sit under 10% crossing are the only ones a protein-only version of the report can stand behind without further mitigation. That set spans cardiovascular, immune, joint, and lung diseases, three of the seven body systems, and four of the eight cancer processes.
