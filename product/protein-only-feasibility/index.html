Context

Checking the feasibility estimate against live data

The Protein-Only Product Feasibility Analysis estimated that a protein-only assay would retain 70–80% of report value and ~85–90% of risk-detection value, based on a process-level count: whether each of the 37 body-system processes retains at least one protein marker. That's a structural estimate — it counts coverage, not how often each marker actually fires in real patients.

This page runs the same question against biomarker_explorer_full.csv — 460,194 result rows across 1,406 patients and 1,447 barcodes — using the ASSAY_NAME column to split every result into protein (mrm*) or metabolite (myco*, mymet*). Exposome-panel rows (2,664 rows, a separate environmental-toxin assay) are excluded — they're neither protein nor metabolite in the sense the proposal means.

Headline: the marker-count math checks out almost exactly (140 protein markers, matching the proposal's Panel B count). But at the level of what real patients actually see flagged, protein-only is a thinner slice than the process-level estimate implies — see Per-patient signal.
Section 1

What's actually in the panel

Across all reported results, 140 distinct markers run on protein (mrm) assays and 218 distinct markers run on metabolomics (myco/mymet) assays. The protein count lines up exactly with the proposal's Panel B figure. The metabolite count doesn't: the proposal's process-mapped panel uses 81 metabolites, but the live panel actually tests 218 — roughly 137 metabolites are measured and reported but don't feed into the 37 mapped processes the current report copy is built on.

That gap doesn't change the protein-only math directly, but it means the "mappable panel" in the proposal is a curated subset of a broader metabolomics panel already in production — worth knowing before sizing engineering or QC effort for either tier.

Section 2

Real-world flag rates by marker type

Per-result, metabolites flag more often than proteins. Across every result with a level assigned, 13.2% of metabolite results land outside the reference range vs. 9.7% of protein results — and metabolites carry a higher red (critical) rate too, 6.2% vs 4.7%.

Calibration caveat. Section 3

What a real patient would actually see

This is the number that matters for the feasibility call. For each of the 1,406 patients, we counted every yellow/red flag they carry across their available results, split by marker type. Every patient (100%) has at least one flag of some kind — that's expected, given the panel's breadth. The question is how many of those flags survive a protein-only cut.

93.7% of patients (1,317 of 1,406) keep at least one protein-driven flag — consistent with the proposal's "hooks survive" claim. But that's a low bar. Looking at the share of each patient's flags that are protein vs. metabolite, the mean is 41.7% and the median is 37.5% — meaning a typical patient's flagged results are metabolite-majority, not protein-majority.

The two failure modes, in numbers

89 patients (6.3%) have zero protein-driven flags whatsoever — a protein-only report would show them a fully clear result. Every single one of these 89 has at least one metabolite red (critical) flag in their full panel.

152 patients (10.8%) have no protein red flag specifically (they may still have protein yellow flags). Of those, 143 (94%) have a metabolite red flag — meaning for roughly 1 in 10 patients, the single most severe finding in their entire panel disappears under a protein-only cut, even though the report wouldn't necessarily read as "all clear."

This is the risk the process-level estimate doesn't surface. "70% of a disease's processes keep a protein marker" and "10.8% of patients' worst finding disappears" are both true and both real — they're just answering different questions. The proposal's coverage math is a reasonable estimate of structural completeness; this is a measurement of how often that structure actually matters for a given patient.
Section 4

Reconciling the two estimates

The proposal's ~85–90% "actionable clinical value retained" figure comes from counting, per disease, what share of associated processes still have at least one surviving protein marker. That's a binary, per-process test — a process counts as "kept" the moment a single protein marker in it exists, regardless of whether that marker is the one that actually flags for a given patient, or how it compares to the metabolite markers sharing that process.

The patient-level data answers a narrower but more concrete question: of the flags a patient's full panel actually produces, what share would still show up on a protein-only report? The answer — a 37.5% median protein share, and a 10.8% "worst finding disappears" rate — is lower than the process-level estimate would suggest, for two compounding reasons visible in the data: metabolites outnumber proteins in the tested panel (218 vs. 140), and metabolites flag at a higher per-marker rate (13.2% vs. 9.7% OOR).

Neither number is wrong. The process-level estimate is the right frame for "does the report structure survive" (Chapters 1–4 in the proposal). The patient-level number is the right frame for "how often does a specific person's specific result disappear" — which is the number that matters for support tickets, trust, and any claim made in marketing copy.

Verdict

Still feasible — with a quantified, not assumed, gap

The data supports the proposal's core call: protein-only is feasible, the economics are favorable, and the marker-count math (140 protein, matching Panel B exactly) checks out. What changes is the confidence interval on "how much value survives." The process-level estimate (~85–90%) is a ceiling; the patient-level measurement (37.5% median flag share, 89.2% retain their worst finding) is closer to the floor. Reality for any given patient sits somewhere between those two numbers depending on their specific results.

Recommendations:

1. Don't market Core on an aggregate "retains ~90% of value" figure — it's true in the process sense and misleading in the patient sense. Frame Core around what it covers (cardiometabolic, immune, organ-health, as the proposal already suggests), not around a retained-value percentage.

2. Treat the 89 zero-protein-flag patients (6.3%) and the 63 additional no-protein-red patients (4.5%) as a known, sized tradeoff — roughly 1 in 9 Core customers will have their most clinically significant full-panel finding be invisible on a protein-only report. Decide now whether that's acceptable as-is, mitigated with a "your results suggest the Complete panel may be worth it" nudge, or reduced by moving specific high-value metabolites (e.g. the acylcarnitines and short-chain fatty acids driving the highest metabolite flag rates) into Core.

3. Re-run this analysis after the myco 1.2 hqe / mymet 2.0 reference-range calibration issue is resolved — part of the metabolite flag-rate advantage may be a QC artifact rather than durable biology, and the 10.8% figure should be rechecked once that's settled.
