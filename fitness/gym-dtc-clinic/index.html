Context

Why we ran this analysis

The newest batch's QC revealed an unexpectedly high percentage of out-of-range metabolites using the new Met 2.0 reference ranges. The question was whether the elevated OOR rate reflects a genuine biological signal, a cohort composition effect, a sex-driven difference, or an assay and reference range issue.

We analysed 50 barcodes across the last two batches, spanning three acquisition channels: Gym (n=16), DTC (n=25), and Clinic (n=9). Data uses unprocessed lab concentrations per Haiyan's request, meaning all biomarkers are included regardless of their final NR status as a result of QC.

Short answer: The elevated OOR rate is driven primarily by the gym cohort, whose metabolomic profile is systematically higher across lipids and amino acids. This pattern survives adjustment for age and sex (56 of 57 significant biomarkers), making cohort membership the most likely explanatory variable. Reference range calibration for a gym-heavy population warrants review.
Section 1

Who did we analyse?

Demographics are well-balanced across cohorts. Age distributions do not differ significantly across the three groups (Kruskal-Wallis p=0.72), and sex ratios are similar (chi-square p=0.93). Demographic confounding is not a meaningful concern for the cohort comparisons that follow.

Section 2

Out-of-range patterns

Overall OOR rates order as Clinic (18.5%) > DTC (16.9%) > Gym (14.4%), but the direction of those flags tells a more informative story than the overall rate alone. Gym has the lowest overall OOR rate and the highest rate of high readings (10.3%). Low OOR readings are rare in this group (4.2%). Clinic and DTC both skew toward low or deficient readings — 11.9% and 10.0% low OOR respectively — with comparatively lower high-OOR rates.

Each cohort shows a distinct biomarker signature. Gym OOR flags are concentrated in sphingomyelins, ether-linked phosphatidylcholines (PCs), and lyso-PCs, almost entirely from the metabolomics panel. Clinic is dominated by acylcarnitines and several PC lipid species, plus broad complement and coagulation protein flags. DTC shows tyramine, serotonin, fetuin-B, and mannose-binding protein C as the most characteristic markers. The myco 1.2 hqe assay appears exclusively in DTC barcodes at a 26.8% OOR rate — a DTC-specific test protocol difference worth noting.

Section 3

PCA cluster analysis

A PCA of binary out-of-range profiles (one row per barcode, one column per biomarker, 1 = OOR) shows that the three cohorts have distinct molecular structures. PC1 explains 12.2% of variance and PC2 explains 6.5%. Clinic separates clearly at positive PC1, driven by acylcarnitines and PC lipids. Gym clusters at negative PC1, with one notable exception (957B845, see outlier section). DTC spans both axes widely, consistent with a general population sample.

Coloring the same plot by sex reveals no sex-driven clustering. Male and female samples do not separate along either principal component, confirming that sex does not explain the cohort structure.

Section 4

Concentration-based analysis

To test whether cohort differences are driven by genuine concentration differences rather than reference range calibration, we ran Kruskal-Wallis tests across 288 biomarkers using raw concentrations, with Benjamini-Hochberg correction at 5% FDR. We then repeated the analysis after residualising concentrations on age and sex to isolate the cohort signal from demographic variation.

57 of 288 biomarkers differ significantly across cohorts. 56 of those 57 survive age and sex adjustment, meaning the cohort effect is independent of demographics. All 57 significant biomarkers are elevated in Gym relative to the other cohorts. Clinic and DTC are statistically indistinguishable from each other in concentration — the primary contrast is Gym versus the rest.

The significant Gym-elevated markers cluster into ether-linked PCs (approximately 18 hits), diacyl PCs (11), sphingomyelins (5), lyso-PCs (5), and amino acids including creatinine, phenylalanine, tryptophan, lysine, leucine, choline, and kynurenine. Fold changes over DTC range from 1.1× to 2.0×. This pattern is consistent with an exercise and physical activity metabolic signature, though pre-analytical factors such as exercise timing, fasting state, and supplement use at collection cannot be excluded without intake data.

The one biomarker that does not survive adjustment is SM (OH) C22:1, which has a moderate age correlation (Spearman ρ=0.31) and loses significance once age is accounted for. All other findings are age-independent.

Section 5

Sex effects

16 biomarkers differ significantly by sex after FDR correction (Mann-Whitney U + Benjamini-Hochberg at 5%), independent of cohort. All are biologically expected differences. Males are higher in creatinine, branched-chain amino acids (isoleucine, leucine, valine), glutamic acid, alpha-aminoadipic acid, uric acid, and serum amyloid P-component. Females are higher in sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), pregnancy zone protein, leucine-rich alpha-2-glycoprotein 1, alpha-2-macroglobulin, apolipoprotein A-I, complement component C6, SM C16:1, and PC ae C42:3.

These sex differences do not confound the cohort findings. The cohort signal holds after adjusting for sex, and the PCA shows no sex-driven clustering. The sex-significant biomarkers and the cohort-significant biomarkers are largely non-overlapping lists.

Section 6

Outlier: 957B845

One Gym barcode stands out as a strong PCA outlier across all three versions of the dataset. The barcode sits at PC1=−17.7, PC2=+25.3, well outside the main Gym cluster. 84 of 270 assessed biomarkers are flagged OOR (31%). 83 of those 84 are high; only fibronectin is low.

The profile spans both panels: metabolomics OOR flags cover ether-linked PCs (18 species), sphingomyelins (14), diacyl PCs (11), and lyso-PCs (4), alongside amino acids including glutamine, lysine, histidine, and spermine. Proteomics flags include apolipoproteins (A-I, A-II, C-I, E, M), complement proteins (C1s, C8 alpha, C8 beta, factor B), coagulation factors (XII, plasminogen), and acute-phase markers including serum albumin and vitamin D-binding protein.

Two explanations are plausible. A lipemic blood draw can artificially elevate many PC and SM species in metabolomics; this would account for the lipid signal. The proteomics elevation — spanning apolipoproteins, complement, and coagulation — is harder to explain as a pre-analytical artifact and may reflect genuine biology. Recommend sample-level QC review before including this barcode in downstream analyses.
