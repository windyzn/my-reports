Molecular You — Longitudinal Case Study

Ten Years of Data,
One Patient's Health Story

Subject: Robert Fraser DOB: 1963-06-04 13 tests  ·  2015–2026 MY ID: MY2228526
Published: 2026-06-24 Last edited: 2026-06-24

Since his first test in 2015, Rob has built one of the longest continuous molecular health records in our dataset. The record shows how his health has changed, which signals responded to intervention, and what his molecular profile actually looks like when something goes acutely wrong. When Rob tested in January 2026 during an episode of active infection, his results were unambiguous in context. Without five prior tests establishing his baseline, the same data would have been substantially harder to interpret.

Longitudinal testing adds context, and context is what turns a biomarker flag into a clinical insight.

Section 01

Testing Timeline

Rob has tested 13 times across a decade through four clinics and projects. The first six visits predate our current panel infrastructure; collection dates are known but molecular data are not available. The six tests from 2021 onward form the analytical record examined here, covering Rob's late 50s into his early 60s.

Section 02

Across five stable tests from 2021 to 2025, Rob's green biomarker rate held above 89% and his flagged condition count fell from 25 to 0. The January 2026 test departs sharply from this pattern, with red conditions rising from 0 to 23 in a single interval. That stable baseline is what makes the contrast interpretable.

Section 04

Key Biomarker Trajectories

Biomarker scores (0–100 scale) across all six tests reveal two distinct patterns. Several biomarkers improved over the stable period and held — kynurenine, spermidine, and apolipoprotein A-I each moved from flagged or suboptimal in 2021 toward optimal by 2023 and stayed there. SM (OH) C24:1 shows a within-period decline that the stable average would obscure. A second group — CRP, haptoglobin, fibrinogen alpha chain, and alpha-1-acid glycoprotein 1 — held at 100 across five consecutive tests before dropping sharply in January 2026. The specifics of that episode are covered in Section 05.

The January 2026 test was collected during an episode of active infection. The molecular signature is specific: CRP at 312.9 nM against a reference range of 0–26.9 nM, haptoglobin at 41027 nM against an upper reference of 19786 nM, both fibrinogen chains elevated, and serum amyloid proteins A-1 and A-4 both above range. These findings represent coordinated activation of the liver's acute-phase protein program alongside simultaneous engagement of the coagulation cascade and the complement system.

The condition scores reflect the same pattern. Inflammation response fell from 100 to 17. Innate immunity dropped from 100 to 25. Rheumatoid arthritis risk, which had held at 96–100 across five tests, dropped to 22 — driven entirely by the acute-phase protein elevation rather than any structural joint disease. Blood clot formation scored 38. Recovery scored 51.

What held up is equally informative. Amino acid conditions remained green. Liver disease scored 100. Neurotransmitter metabolism, kidney filtration, glycolysis, and hormone transport all stayed above 91. The collapse was concentrated in the immune and coagulation systems, not distributed across Rob's full metabolic profile. That specificity reflects a body under acute immune stress with intact metabolic reserves.

The report published on February 23, 2026 identified rheumatoid arthritis (score 22), depression (48), and COPD (49) as the top disease risks — each driven by the shared acute-phase signal rather than independent pathological processes. Without the longitudinal baseline, these scores would be harder to contextualise. With it, the acute and transient nature of the pattern is evident.

Section 06

What the Full Record Shows

Across nearly five years of data, a few patterns stand out that no single test could have identified. Some signals improved and held. Some persisted without resolution. One arrived suddenly and sharply, then resolved against a background of stability that made its significance clear.

Several conditions resolved and stayed resolved. Blood vessel lining health, germ detection, vascular inflammation, brain and nerve signals, innate immunity, and tissue and organ structural support all moved from flagged in 2021 to 100 by 2022, and held there across all subsequent tests. These are sustained shifts visible only because Rob kept testing.
Diacetylspermine was below the detection limit in every stable test. Flagged red in Mar 2021, Feb 2022, Feb 2023, and Aug 2023 without change. Not measured in the 2025 or 2026 panels. Four consecutive readings at the floor of detection is a different clinical picture from a single flag.
Apolipoprotein A-I was consistently above its upper reference range across 2022 and 2023. 76921 nM (Feb 2022), 73455 nM (Feb 2023), 71276 nM (Aug 2023) — all above reference, across three independent tests. Substantially lower in Oct 2025 (28288 nM). The stable-period pattern has not been explained by any single test in isolation.
SM (OH) C24:1 and SM (OH) C22:2 both declined within a single year. Both sphingolipids were normal in 2021 and 2022, then fell to red by Feb 2023 and continued declining through Aug 2023. Neither was measured in 2025 or 2026, leaving the trajectory open. A within-year directional shift in two related lipids is a finding that requires longitudinal data to detect.
The January 2026 episode was acute, not gradual. October 2025 showed a clean profile — 0 red conditions, 3 flagged biomarkers. Three months later, 23 conditions were red and 25 biomarkers were flagged. The transition was discrete and the molecular pattern was specific. A single test in January 2026 without the October 2025 baseline would have been substantially harder to interpret as acute rather than chronic.
